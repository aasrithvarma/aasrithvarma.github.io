Living with Congenital Heart Disease: A Parent's Guide to Everyday Care

Dr. Tanmai Aasrith Varma Ayenampudi 1
CliN ID: CN-2026.e7ad50

Receiving a diagnosis of your kid's congenital heart disease (CHD) can be one of the most overwhelming moments for a parent. It is natural to wonder whether your child will be able to eat normally, go to school, play with friends, travel, or live an active life.

The good news is that most children with congenital heart disease grow up to lead healthy, fulfilling lives. Advances in diagnosis, surgery, catheter-based interventions, and long-term medical care have dramatically improved outcomes over the past few decades. While every child's heart condition is unique, understanding how to care for your child in everyday situations can help you feel more confident and prepared.

This guide answers common questions parents ask about daily life with congenital heart disease.

Feeding and Nutrition

Good nutrition is one of the most important parts of your child's growth and development. However, some children with heart disease burn more calories than other children because their heart has to work harder. Others may tire easily during feeding, making it difficult to take enough milk or food.

Infants

Breastfeeding is encouraged whenever possible. Breast milk provides excellent nutrition and immune protection. Some babies with significant heart disease may become tired before completing a feed. In these situations, your healthcare team may recommend:

  • Smaller, more frequent feeds
  • Fortified breast milk
  • High-calorie infant formula
  • Feeding through a nasogastric (NG - a tube that goes into the stomach from their nose) tube for a period of time if necessary

Remember that feeding should generally be a pleasant experience. If every feed becomes exhausting for your baby, discuss this with your pediatric cardiologist.

Older Children

Once your child begins solid foods, encourage a balanced diet that includes:

  • Fruits and vegetables
  • Whole grains
  • Lean sources of protein
  • Plant-based food
  • Healthy fats

There is no special "heart disease diet" for most children with congenital heart disease. Unless specifically advised by your cardiologist, your child does not need unnecessary dietary restrictions.

Children who have heart failure or certain complex heart conditions may occasionally require limits on salt or fluids, but these recommendations are individualized.

When should you contact your doctor?

If your child:

  • Sweats excessively during feeds
  • Takes longer than 30-40 minutes to finish most feeds
  • Breathes rapidly while eating
  • Turns blue during feeding
  • Frequently vomits after feeds
  • Is not gaining weight appropriately

Physical Activity and Exercise

Many parents worry that physical activity might harm their child's heart. In fact, regular exercise is beneficial for most children with congenital heart disease. It improves cardiovascular fitness, muscle strength, confidence, emotional well-being, and overall quality of life.

For many children with repaired or mild congenital heart disease, normal childhood activities including running, cycling, swimming, playground games, and school sports are perfectly safe.

Some children with more complex heart conditions, pulmonary hypertension, significant arrhythmias, or reduced heart function may require individualized exercise recommendations. Your pediatric cardiologist will advise you if certain activities should be limited.

Encourage your child to remain active rather than overprotecting them. Many parents unintentionally restrict activity out of fear, but unnecessary limitations can affect physical fitness and social development.

Stop exercise and seek medical advice if your child develops:

  • Chest pain
  • Dizziness or fainting
  • Palpitations
  • Severe shortness of breath
  • Blue discoloration that is unusual for your child

School and Learning

Most children with congenital heart disease attend regular school and participate in classroom activities just like their peers.

Some children may miss school because of medical appointments, surgeries, or hospital admissions. Others may become tired more easily during the school day.

It is helpful to inform teachers and school staff about your child's condition, including:

  • Their diagnosis
  • Current medications
  • Any activity restrictions
  • Emergency contact numbers
  • What to do if your child becomes unwell

Children with complex heart disease or prolonged hospitalizations may occasionally need additional educational support, but many perform exceptionally well academically.

Vaccinations

Children with congenital heart disease are particularly vulnerable to complications from respiratory infections. Keeping vaccinations up to date is one of the simplest ways to protect your child.

Routine childhood immunizations should be given according to the national immunization schedule unless your doctor advises otherwise.

Additional vaccines may include:

  • Annual influenza vaccine
  • Pneumococcal vaccination (when indicated)
  • RSV preventive therapies for eligible infants
  • COVID-19 vaccination according to current recommendations

If your child is scheduled for heart surgery, discuss vaccination timing with your healthcare team.

Dental Care

Good dental health is closely linked to heart health.

Poor oral hygiene can increase the risk of bacteria entering the bloodstream during dental disease or procedures. In certain children with congenital heart disease, this can lead to infective endocarditis, a serious infection of the heart lining or valves.

Parents should encourage:

  • Brushing teeth twice daily
  • Regular dental check-ups
  • Limiting sugary snacks and drinks

Some children require antibiotics before certain dental procedures, but not every child with congenital heart disease needs this. Your cardiologist can advise whether antibiotic prophylaxis is recommended.

Sleep

Most children with congenital heart disease sleep normally.

However, poor sleep may sometimes indicate an underlying problem. Seek medical advice if your child has:

  • Persistent snoring
  • Difficulty breathing during sleep
  • Frequent pauses in breathing
  • Excessive sweating at night
  • Worsening fatigue during the day

Adequate sleep is important for growth, learning, and heart health.

Travel

Most children with stable congenital heart disease can travel safely.

Before traveling:

  • Carry enough medications for the entire trip
  • Keep a copy of your child's medical records and medication list
  • Know the location of nearby medical facilities
  • Ensure appropriate travel insurance if required

Discuss travel plans with your cardiologist if your child has:

  • Cyanotic heart disease
  • Pulmonary hypertension
  • Recent heart surgery
  • Oxygen dependence
  • Severe heart failure

Air travel is safe for many children but may require additional planning in these situations.

Everyday Illnesses

Children with congenital heart disease still get common childhood illnesses such as colds, coughs, stomach infections, and fevers.

During illness:

  • Encourage fluids to prevent dehydration (unless indicated otherwise).
  • Continue medications unless your doctor advises otherwise.
  • Monitor breathing and feeding closely.
  • Treat fever appropriately using medications recommended by your doctor.

Seek medical attention earlier than usual if your child develops:

  • Persistent high fever
  • Difficulty breathing
  • Poor feeding
  • Reduced urine output
  • Worsening blue discoloration
  • Excessive sleepiness

Emotional Well-being

Living with a heart condition affects the whole family... not just the child.

Children may feel anxious before hospital visits or worry about being different from their friends. Parents may experience ongoing stress and uncertainty.

Helping your child live as normally as possible can build confidence and resilience.

Encourage your child to:

  • Play with friends
  • Attend school regularly
  • Participate in hobbies
  • Ask questions about their condition in an age-appropriate way

Do not let the heart condition define your child's identity.

Regular Follow-up*

Even if your child feels completely well, regular follow-up with a pediatric cardiologist remains essential.*

Some congenital heart conditions require lifelong monitoring because changes can occur many years after surgery or treatment.

Never stop prescribed medications without discussing them with your healthcare provider, and attend all scheduled appointments.

Warning Signs: When Should You Seek Immediate Medical Care?

Contact your doctor immediately or go to the nearest emergency department if your child develops:

  • Severe difficulty breathing
  • Blue lips or tongue that do not improve or are worse than usual
  • A cyanotic (Tet) spell
  • Loss of consciousness or fainting
  • Seizures
  • Persistent chest pain
  • Palpitations associated with dizziness or collapse
  • Extreme lethargy or difficulty waking
  • Poor feeding in infants with rapid breathing
  • Swelling of the face, abdomen, or legs
  • Significantly reduced urine output
  • Persistent vomiting preventing medications from being taken
Key Takeaways

Although congenital heart disease presents unique challenges, it does not prevent most children from enjoying a meaningful and active childhood. With appropriate nutrition, regular exercise, routine vaccinations, good dental care, and ongoing follow-up, many children with CHD thrive at home, at school, and in their communities.

As a parent, you do not need to have all the answers, but learning about your child's condition, recognizing warning signs, and maintaining a close partnership with your healthcare team will help you provide the best possible care. Every child with congenital heart disease is unique, and your pediatric cardiologist is the best source of advice tailored to your child's specific diagnosis and needs.
