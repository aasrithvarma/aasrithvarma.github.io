Why Do People Get Motion Sickness? The Science Behind That Nauseous Feeling

Dr. Tanmai Aasrith Varma Ayenampudi 1
CliN ID: CN-2026.342c82

"Why do I feel sick in a car, but my friend enjoys the ride? Why can I read a book at home without any problem, but the moment I start reading in a moving bus, I feel nauseous?"

If you've ever asked these questions, you're not alone. Motion sickness affects millions of people every year, whether they're traveling by car, bus, train, airplane, or boat. Some even experience it while using virtual reality (VR) headsets or playing certain video games. Although it can make even a short journey miserable, motion sickness is not a disease. Instead, it is a fascinating example of how our brain interprets information from different parts of the body.

Your Brain Is Constantly Trying to Understand Movement

Every second of the day, your brain is asking a simple question:

"Am I moving?"

To answer this, it relies on three different sources of information. Your eyes tell the brain what they can see. Your inner ears, specifically the vestibular system, detect changes in movement, acceleration, and balance. Finally, sensors in your muscles and joints, known as the proprioceptive system, inform the brain about the position of your body and limbs.

When all three systems agree, everything works perfectly. Imagine walking through a park. Your eyes see the scenery moving past you, your inner ear detects your movement, and your muscles feel your legs walking. The brain receives matching information and correctly understands that you are moving.

So, Why Does Motion Sickness Happen?

Motion sickness develops when these three systems disagree with each other. This is known as the sensory conflict theory, the most widely accepted explanation for motion sickness.

Imagine sitting in the back seat of a moving car while reading a book. Your eyes are focused on the stationary pages in front of you, so they tell your brain that you are sitting still. At the same time, your inner ear detects every acceleration, brake, turn, and bump in the road, sending strong signals that your body is moving. Meanwhile, your muscles report that you are simply sitting in one place.

Your brain suddenly receives three different stories. One system says you're moving, another says you're not, and a third says you're sitting still. Unable to reconcile these conflicting messages, the brain responds by triggering the symptoms we know as motion sickness.

Why Does It Cause Nausea?

One of the most interesting theories behind motion sickness dates back to our evolutionary past.

Scientists believe that when the brain receives conflicting sensory information, it may mistakenly interpret it as the effect of a toxin affecting the nervous system. Since certain poisons can cause hallucinations and altered perception, the brain may activate its protective mechanism to remove the suspected toxin from the body.

This response leads to symptoms such as:

  • Nausea
  • Cold sweating
  • Dizziness
  • Vomiting
  • Excessive salivation

Although this "poison theory" has not been definitively proven, it remains one of the most accepted explanations for why motion sickness often results in vomiting.

Why Does Reading Make It Worse?

This is perhaps the most common example of sensory conflict.

When you're reading inside a moving vehicle, your eyes remain fixed on a page that isn't moving. As far as your eyes are concerned, everything is perfectly still. However, your inner ear is constantly sensing movement as the vehicle accelerates, turns corners, or travels over uneven roads.

Because the information from your eyes and inner ears no longer matches, your brain becomes confused. The greater this mismatch, the more severe the symptoms may become.

This is also why many people can comfortably look out of the window during a journey but start feeling sick within minutes of looking down at a phone or book.

Why Are Boats So Much Worse?

If you've ever experienced seasickness, you know it can be far more intense than car sickness.

Unlike cars, which mostly move forward with occasional turns, boats move continuously in several directions at once. They pitch, roll, sway, and rise with the waves. These slow, unpredictable movements constantly stimulate the vestibular system, making it much harder for the brain to adapt.

Even experienced sailors can develop seasickness when the sea becomes particularly rough.

Why Do Some People Get Motion Sickness More Easily?

Not everyone experiences motion sickness to the same degree. Some people can read throughout a winding mountain journey without any discomfort, while others begin to feel nauseous within minutes.

Several factors increase the likelihood of motion sickness, including:

  • Being between 2 and 12 years of age
  • Having migraine headaches
  • Pregnancy
  • A family history of motion sickness
  • Anxiety or previous unpleasant travel experiences

Interestingly, infants younger than about two years rarely develop motion sickness because the parts of the brain responsible for integrating these sensory signals are still developing. Many adults also notice that motion sickness becomes less severe with age as the brain gradually adapts to repeated exposure.

What Are the Symptoms?

Motion sickness usually develops gradually rather than suddenly. Most people first notice a vague feeling that something isn't quite right before more obvious symptoms appear.

Common symptoms include:

  • Nausea
  • Dizziness
  • Cold sweating
  • Pale skin
  • Excessive salivation
  • Headache
  • Yawning
  • Fatigue
  • Vomiting

The severity varies from person to person and may depend on the duration and intensity of the motion.

Can Motion Sickness Be Prevented?

While motion sickness cannot always be prevented, a few simple measures can significantly reduce the chances of developing symptoms.

Looking outside at the horizon is one of the most effective strategies because it allows your eyes to see the same movement that your inner ear is detecting. This helps eliminate the sensory conflict.

If you're traveling by car, sitting in the front passenger seat is generally better than sitting in the back. On an airplane, seats over the wings experience less movement, while on a ship, the middle of the vessel is usually the most stable.

It is also helpful to avoid reading books or using mobile phones during travel if you are prone to motion sickness. Keeping your head as still as possible, staying hydrated, eating a light meal before travel, and ensuring good ventilation or fresh air can also reduce symptoms.

Do Medications Help?

Yes. Several medications are available to prevent or reduce motion sickness, particularly for people who travel frequently or have severe symptoms.

Commonly used medications include:

  • Meclizine
  • Dimenhydrinate
  • Promethazine
  • Hyoscine Hydrobromide (Scopolamine, not to be confused with Hyoscine Butylbromide or Buscogast)

These medicines are generally most effective when taken before the journey begins, as preventing motion sickness is easier than treating it once symptoms have already started. Since some of these medications can cause drowsiness, they should be used according to a healthcare professional's advice.

Is Motion Sickness Dangerous?

Fortunately, motion sickness itself is not dangerous. Although it can be extremely unpleasant, it usually resolves once the motion stops.

However, prolonged vomiting can lead to dehydration, particularly in young children, older adults, and pregnant women. If symptoms are unusually severe, persist long after the journey has ended, or occur even when you are not moving, medical evaluation is recommended to rule out conditions affecting the inner ear or nervous system.

Motion sickness is a remarkable example of how the brain depends on teamwork between the eyes, inner ears, and the body's sensory system. When these systems send conflicting information... as happens while reading in a moving car or traveling on a rough sea... the brain struggles to determine whether the body is actually moving. This sensory mismatch triggers nausea, dizziness, and sometimes vomiting.

Although motion sickness can make travel uncomfortable, it is usually harmless. Understanding why it occurs can help you manage it more effectively and appreciate the remarkable complexity of the human brain. The next time someone says they "get car sick," you'll know that the problem isn't really in their stomach... it's the brain trying to solve a sensory puzzle.
